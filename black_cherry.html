Black Cherry

Black cherry (Prunus serotina) is a North American native fruit tree producing small, dark purple to nearly black cherries. It is valued both for its fruit and its high-quality timber.

Description

Black cherries are small, round fruits that turn deep red to black when fully ripe. The flesh is juicy but slightly astringent, and each fruit contains a single hard seed.

Distribution and Habitat

Black cherry is found in:

  • Eastern United States
  • Canada
  • Mexico (northern regions)

It grows in forests, woodlands, and along streams in temperate climates.

Cultivation

The tree prefers:

  • Well-drained soil
  • Full sun to partial shade
  • Temperate climates

It is often grown as a wild or semi-cultivated species rather than intensive orchard crop.

Uses

Black cherry is used for:

  • Jams and jellies
  • Pies and desserts
  • Flavoring syrups and liqueurs
  • Wildlife food source

Nutritional Value

Black cherries contain:

  • Vitamin C
  • Antioxidants (anthocyanins)
  • Fiber
  • Natural sugars

Interesting Facts

  • The bark of the tree is also used in traditional medicine.
  • It is one of the largest native cherry species in North America.
  • Birds heavily rely on its fruit as a food source.
