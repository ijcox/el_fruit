Barbadine

Barbadine (Passiflora quadrangularis), also known as giant granadilla, is one of the largest species of passion fruit. It is native to tropical South America and is widely grown in the Caribbean and other tropical regions for its aromatic, mildly sweet pulp.

Description

Barbadine fruits are large, oblong, and can grow up to 12 inches long. The outer skin is thick and green when unripe, turning slightly yellowish as it matures. Inside, the fruit contains a soft, aromatic pulp with black seeds surrounded by a gelatinous texture.

Distribution and Habitat

Barbadine is found in:

  • Caribbean islands
  • Central and South America
  • Southeast Asia
  • Tropical Africa

It thrives in warm, humid tropical environments and is often grown on trellises or fences due to its vine-like nature.

Cultivation

Barbadine vines require:

  • Full sun to partial shade
  • Well-drained, fertile soil
  • Support structures for climbing

The plant grows rapidly and produces large fruits within a year or two under ideal conditions.

Uses

Barbadine is used in a variety of ways:

  • Fresh pulp consumption
  • Juices and beverages
  • Desserts and sorbets
  • Jams and preserves

In some regions, the young fruit is also cooked as a vegetable.

Nutritional Value

Barbadine contains:

  • Vitamin C
  • Vitamin A
  • Dietary fiber
  • Antioxidants

Interesting Facts

  • It is one of the largest fruits in the passionfruit family.
  • The vine is also ornamental due to its large purple flowers.
  • Both fruit and young leaves are used in traditional remedies.
